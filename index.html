Chat2Markdown

Chat2Markdown

[video demo below]

chat2markdown is an experimental chat app that allows you and your friends to modify all messages in the chat… all for the sake of a good markdown file.

Philosophical assumptions:

  • data shouldn’t exist in silos
  • chats are collective artifacts
  • having all the knobs of a system is good sometimes

I built this app during my Software Engineering internship at Meta Platforms this summer. It was my very first time making an app and learning Objective-C.

This project taught me many things. First, it taught me how to work in a semi-independent way. I got constant feedback from my manager and a project peer and worked hard to cultivate a harmonious, fun, and helpful relationship.

Most importantly, however, I learned a lot about dealing with the arising complexity of programming. In fact, because the app worked as a regular messaging app while still allowing messages to be manipulated in all possible ways by both users in real-time conversation, I ran into many edge cases that forced me to redesign my data models multiple times.

Putting on my self-critic hat, I think the app could have been better visually polished. It also still contains some bugs lurking around. This was largely due to the fact that I only spent about 6 weeks coding it, having started the internship with zero experience in iOS (and mobile development in general).

Nevertheless, it was truly empowering to execute such a weird and quirky idea like this. I was genuinely motivated by giving life to the philosophical assumptions behind the app, giving birth to a new medium that no one had ventured to try before.

Exemplio

Exemplio

exemplio was an educational platform aimed at high school students in Brazil. Its value proposition was simple: to compile, dissect and disseminate stories of diverse role models to encourage young people. To do so, we had a page for each "example," containing a podcast episode and a graphical timeline of their personal journey.

exemplio lived at https://exempl.io (RIP) and contained around 20 "examples" of students. Its design was highly experimental—we even had to restart the project from scratch after having launched it for a couple of months. There were no interfaces like these, but I got a bit of inspiration from the design of Headspace.

The idea was cool, and I learned much from managing and shaping a project like this. In retrospect, though, I don't think we've ever reached the scale we wanted. The premise of democratizing role models was pretty cool, but interviewing, adding descriptions, and generating code for each graphical timeline was too time-consuming, further preventing scale. We thought of "decentralizing" the process, allowing signed users to record their own personal journeys and input their timelines, but none of us were quite technically there back then.

"Officially," I was considered a Head of Product. Which basically involved doing a little bit of all, like editing all podcasts, writing descriptions for each example, and — of course — helping to build the technical part of the project. Technically, I ideated all the UI/UX design using Adobe XD and contributed to the code using ReactJS, Javascript, SQL, CSS, and HTML.

Cold Turkey Blocker workflow

Cold Turkey Blocker workflow


A huge chunk of the interactions I have with my computer happens through the Alfred Powerpack. I use it on average 140 times a day—triggering custom snippets, workflows and keywords.

One of the ways I use is as my own kind of programmable attention system. To do so, I trigger blocks in the Cold Turkey Blocker app—preventing me from accessing a collection of websites for a given amount of time.

I built and published a Cold Turkey workflow for alfred, and it can be downloaded here.

Towards a generalized model of information seeking

This "Research-based argument" paper was the final assignment for a writing requirement class. I wrote it during the fall quarter of my sophomore year. Back then, I was obsessed with the concept of information and how we humans can develop virtuoso skills to find it effectively.

Unfortunately, I hadn't come across Michael Nielsen's writings back then. If I had done so, I would have claimed that part of this work was about finding a way to encourage users to internalize the cognitive elements of different information retrieval systems effectively.

I wrote this article after my freshman-year summer internship at Dwelo. I sought to describe the metacognitive processes I myself was going through when seeking information. As you may suspect, this writing motivated the previous work I've shown.

It was inspired both by my journey in achieving gold medals in national scientific olympiads as well as my experience with teaching physics for free for other public school students.

Personal Systems

Personal Sytems

There's an obvious yet overlooked character about the fact that we are all systems existing within and around systems. As the months go by, I have been increasingly embodying the identity of being a "self engineer"—designing both my own personal systems and the interfaces with outer systems. Here are a few of my current physical ones

The Wisdom Wall

I was inspired by my friend Sam, from Harvard University, whom I met during my internship at Meta. Sam has over 1000 post-its with written pieces of wisdom glued on his wall—plus a digital version in Google sheets, in case he loses some or needs to recreate them. One interesting function the Wisdom Wall has for him is acting as a resiliency mechanism. So whenever life beats him up, he finds guidance in the pieces of wisdom he left on the wall.

This curiously connects a lot with your take on peripheral vision, especially because the wall is highly dynamic. This dynamism especially shows up whenever one of the post-its inevitably falls down, offering a moment to read it with care before putting it back on the wall.

Based on Sam—and modifying it my way—I have also been curating my own wisdom wall:

Objects queue

Here I put here all objects I want to eventually bring to another room. Usually, this means mugs and bottles I feel too lazy to bring to the kitchen at the moment.

Variety absorption

Variety is a cybernetic concept that means "all possible states of a system." When I don't know where to put an object or am just too lazy to put it in the right place, it goes right into variety absorption.

Cache

The most retrieved physical objects.

Shower in mirror

I often shave while showering, but I always have to fix the untrimmed parts in the sink. Gluing a small mirror in the shower fixes this. I apply a layer of dried soap on the mirror to prevent it from fogging.

The metasystemic hub

Still trying to make sense of this one. I mostly use it as my interface for don't-break-the-chain habit trackers.

Standing desk
